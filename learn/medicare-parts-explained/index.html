Medicare Parts A, B, C, and D — explained in plain English

The four parts of Medicare in one clear map: what each covers, what it costs, and how they fit together so you can choose with confidence.

By the Everything Senior Insurance Data Desk · Reviewed by Christian Brindle, Founder & Licensed Insurance Advisor · Updated 2026

The four parts at a glance

Medicare is built from four parts. Part A covers hospital stays. Part B covers doctor visits and outpatient care. Together, A and B are called Original Medicare. Part C (Medicare Advantage) is a private all-in-one alternative that bundles A, B, and usually D. Part D covers prescription drugs.

You'll generally choose one of two paths: Original Medicare (A + B) usually paired with a Part D drug plan and often a Medigap supplement — or a Medicare Advantage plan (Part C) that rolls it together.

  • Part A — hospital insurance (most people pay $0 premium)
  • Part B — medical insurance ($202.90/mo standard, 2026)
  • Part C — Medicare Advantage, a private bundle
  • Part D — prescription drug coverage

What Part A and Part B cost in 2026

Most people get premium-free Part A because they paid Medicare taxes while working. Part A still has a hospital deductible of $1,736 per benefit period in 2026. Part B has a standard premium of $202.90 per month and a $283 annual deductible in 2026; higher earners pay more through IRMAA.

These numbers change every year, so always confirm the current figures at Medicare.gov before you enroll.

Which path is right for you?

There's no universally 'best' choice — it depends on your doctors, your prescriptions, your budget, and how you like to get care. Medigap offers freedom and predictability at a higher premium; Medicare Advantage offers low premiums and extras in exchange for using a network. A licensed advisor can map your specifics to the right path in about 20 minutes.
