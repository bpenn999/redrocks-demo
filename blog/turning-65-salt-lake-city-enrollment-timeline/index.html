Turning 65 in Salt Lake City? Your Complete 2026 Medicare Enrollment Timeline

A month-by-month Medicare enrollment roadmap for Salt Lake City and Sandy — your exact windows, the penalties you're avoiding, and a local checklist.

By the Everything Senior Insurance Data Desk · Reviewed by Christian Brindle, Founder & Licensed Insurance Advisor · Published July 9, 2026 · Updated July 11, 2026

Key takeaways

  • Your Initial Enrollment Period (IEP) is a 7-month window: 3 months before your 65th birthday month, your birthday month, and 3 months after.
  • Enroll in the first 3 months of the window and coverage starts the month you turn 65 — no gap.
  • Delaying Part B without other creditable coverage adds 10% to your premium for every full 12 months delayed — for life. On 2026's $202.90 standard premium, one year's delay costs about $20.29/month forever.
  • The Part D late penalty is 1% of the national base premium ($38.99 in 2026) per month without creditable drug coverage — about 39 cents/month per month missed, permanently.
  • Your one-time Medigap Open Enrollment (6 months from your Part B effective date) is when supplements can't turn you down or charge more for health history.

The window that starts everything: your IEP

If you're turning 65 in Salt Lake City or Sandy, your first chance to enroll in Medicare is the Initial Enrollment Period (IEP) — a 7-month window built around your 65th birthday: the 3 months before your birthday month, your birthday month itself, and the 3 months after.

Timing inside the window matters. Enroll during the first three months and your coverage begins the first day of your birthday month, with no gap. Enroll during your birthday month or later and your start date slides — which can leave you weeks without coverage you thought you had. The clean move is simple: start the process about 90 days out.

  • Months 1–3 (before your birthday month): enroll now → coverage starts on time
  • Month 4 (your birthday month): coverage starts the following month
  • Months 5–7 (after): coverage start continues to slide — avoid if you can
  • Born on the 1st of a month? Your whole window shifts a month earlier

Month-by-month: what to do when

Here's the timeline we walk Salt Lake City clients through:

  • 6 months out — Confirm how you'll get retiree health coverage; ask HR how your employer plan coordinates with Medicare
  • 4 months out — Gather your doctor list, medication list (with doses), and preferred pharmacy
  • 3 months out — Your IEP opens: enroll in Parts A & B through ssa.gov (or by phone/office); decide your path — Original Medicare + Medigap + Part D, or Medicare Advantage
  • 2 months out — Compare specific plans against your doctor/drug lists; check networks (Intermountain, University of Utah Health) and formularies
  • 1 month out — Enroll in your chosen plan(s); confirm ID cards and effective dates
  • Birthday month — Coverage begins; fill a prescription and book a visit to confirm everything works
  • Within 6 months of Part B — Your one-time Medigap Open Enrollment runs; if you want a supplement, this is the guaranteed-acceptance window

Still working at 65? Read this before you delay anything

Plenty of Utahns work past 65, and if you (or your spouse) have group health coverage from an employer with 20 or more employees, you can usually delay Part B — and Part D, if the drug coverage is creditable — without penalty, then enroll later through a Special Enrollment Period when the employment or coverage ends.

But the rules have teeth. Employers under 20 employees generally can't substitute for Medicare — Medicare becomes primary at 65 and skipping Part B can leave you functionally uninsured. COBRA and retiree coverage do not count as active employment coverage for delaying Part B. And Health Savings Account contributions must stop once any part of Medicare starts (Part A enrollment is backdated up to 6 months, which trips up HSA savers regularly). Before you delay, get your exact situation confirmed — a wrong assumption here is one of the most expensive mistakes in all of Medicare.

The penalties, with real math

The late-enrollment penalties aren't one-time fees — they're permanent surcharges, which is why timing deserves respect.

Part B: delay enrollment without creditable employer coverage and Medicare adds 10% to your premium for each full 12-month period you should have been enrolled. Against 2026's standard $202.90 premium, a one-year delay adds roughly $20.29 every month, for the rest of your life. A three-year delay adds about $60.87 a month — over $730 a year, forever.

Part D: go 63 or more days without creditable drug coverage after your IEP and the penalty is 1% of the national base beneficiary premium ($38.99 in 2026) for every month you went without — added to your drug plan premium for as long as you have Part D. Fifteen uncovered months ≈ $5.85 added per month. The fix is cheap: even a low-premium drug plan keeps you penalty-free while your needs are simple.

Your fork in the road: two ways to get Medicare

During your IEP you'll choose between two paths, and it's easier when you know what each optimizes for.

Original Medicare + Medigap + Part D optimizes for freedom and predictability: see any doctor in the country that accepts Medicare, no referrals, very stable out-of-pocket costs — in exchange for a monthly Medigap premium. Medicare Advantage (Part C) optimizes for low premiums and bundled extras: 17 of Salt Lake County's 24 MA-PD plans are $0-premium for 2026, most include drug coverage plus dental/vision/hearing — in exchange for using a network and paying copays up to an out-of-pocket maximum ($3,500–$9,250 locally in 2026).

Neither is 'best' — CMS forbids that word for good reason. Travelers and snowbirds often lean Medigap; people who get care locally and want the lowest monthly outlay often lean Advantage. Your health, budget, and doctors decide.

The window people regret missing: Medigap Open Enrollment

If the supplement path appeals to you, one deadline outranks the rest: your Medigap Open Enrollment Period — the 6 months starting the month you're 65+ and enrolled in Part B. During this one-time window, insurers must accept you and cannot charge more based on your health history.

After it closes, most Medigap applications in Utah go through medical underwriting — carriers can decline you or price you up for pre-existing conditions. People who choose Advantage at 65 and later want to switch to a supplement sometimes discover the door is no longer wide open. That doesn't make Advantage wrong; it means the Medigap decision deserves a deliberate yes-or-no during your first six months, not a shrug.

Your Salt Lake City checklist

Turning 65 along the Wasatch Front? This is the short list we'd hand you:

  • Confirm your IEP dates (use our free Eligibility & IEP checker in the Tools section)
  • Decide whether you're delaying (only with qualifying employer coverage — verify, don't assume)
  • Gather doctors, prescriptions with doses, and your pharmacy
  • Choose your path: Original + Medigap + Part D, or Medicare Advantage
  • Verify your Intermountain / University of Utah Health providers against any plan you're considering
  • Enroll early in the window so coverage starts the month you turn 65
  • Calendar your Medigap Open Enrollment deadline — 6 months from your Part B effective date

Frequently asked questions

When should I sign up for Medicare if I'm turning 65?

During your 7-month Initial Enrollment Period — ideally in the 3 months before your birthday month, so coverage starts the month you turn 65 with no gap.

Can I delay Medicare if I'm still working at 65?

Often yes — if you have creditable group coverage from an employer with 20+ employees, you can delay Part B penalty-free and enroll later via a Special Enrollment Period. Under-20-employee coverage, COBRA, and retiree plans generally don't qualify. Verify before delaying.

How big is the Part B late-enrollment penalty?

10% of the premium for each full 12 months you delayed without creditable coverage — permanently. Against 2026's $202.90 standard premium, each year of delay costs about $20.29 more per month for life.

How is the Part D late penalty calculated?

1% of the national base beneficiary premium ($38.99 in 2026) multiplied by your months without creditable drug coverage, rounded to the nearest 10 cents, added to your premium for as long as you have Part D.

What is Medigap Open Enrollment and why does it matter?

It's the 6-month window starting when you're 65+ with Part B, during which Medigap insurers must accept you regardless of health history. After it closes, most applications face medical underwriting in Utah.

Should I pick Medicare Advantage or Medigap at 65?

It depends on how you get care. Advantage offers low premiums and extras with networks and copays; Medigap costs more monthly but gives nationwide doctor freedom and predictable bills. An advisor can map your doctors, drugs, and budget to the better fit — free.
