Choosing a Medicare Advantage Plan in Salt Lake County: The Complete 2026 Guide

Salt Lake County has 41 Medicare Advantage plans for 2026 — 17 of the 24 MA-PD plans at $0 premium. A Sandy-based advisor's complete guide to choosing well.

By the Everything Senior Insurance Data Desk · Reviewed by Christian Brindle, Founder & Licensed Insurance Advisor · Published July 11, 2026

Key takeaways

  • Salt Lake County offers 41 Medicare Advantage plans for CY2026: 24 standard MA-PD plans plus 17 Special Needs Plans (SNPs), per the CMS Medicare Plan Landscape.
  • 17 of the 24 MA-PD plans carry a $0 monthly premium — but you still pay your Part B premium ($202.90 standard in 2026) and copays as you use care.
  • In-network out-of-pocket maximums on 2026 Salt Lake County plans range from $3,500 to $9,250 — this number matters more than the premium.
  • The top overall CMS star rating in the county is 4.5 stars, and 11 of the 22 rated plans earn 4 stars or better.
  • The right plan is decided by your doctors, your prescriptions, and your pharmacy — not by the premium. Compare against your own list before you enroll.

How many Medicare Advantage plans are in Salt Lake County for 2026?

For the 2026 plan year, Salt Lake County residents can choose from 41 Medicare Advantage plans, according to the CMS Medicare Plan Landscape: 24 standard Medicare Advantage Prescription Drug (MA-PD) plans plus 17 Special Needs Plans (SNPs) for people with specific chronic conditions or dual Medicare/Medicaid eligibility. That's one of the deepest plan markets in Utah — deeper than Utah County (21 MA-PD plans), Weber County (19), or Grand County (8) for the same year.

Wide choice is genuinely good news: more carriers competing means more $0-premium options, richer extras, and more network combinations. But it also means the odds of picking a mediocre fit by guessing are high. Two plans that look identical on a postcard can treat your specific medications and your specific doctors completely differently.

What do these plans actually cost?

Of the 24 standard MA-PD plans in Salt Lake County, 17 have a $0 monthly plan premium for 2026, and premiums top out at $135 per month. A $0 premium does not mean free healthcare. Everyone with Medicare still pays the Part B premium — $202.90 per month standard in 2026 — and you'll pay copays and coinsurance as you actually use services: a primary care visit, a specialist, an MRI, a hospital stay.

What protects you from a catastrophic year is the plan's in-network maximum out-of-pocket (MOOP) limit. For 2026 Salt Lake County plans, MOOPs range from $3,500 to $9,250. Once your in-network spending hits that ceiling, the plan pays 100% of covered in-network care for the rest of the year. Two $0-premium plans can be $5,750 apart in worst-case exposure — which is why we compare MOOPs before premiums, every time.

Star ratings: what 4.5 stars actually tells you

CMS rates Medicare Advantage plans from 1 to 5 stars each year based on dozens of quality measures — preventive care, chronic condition management, member complaints, customer service responsiveness, and drug safety among them. For 2026, the top overall rating available in Salt Lake County is 4.5 stars, and 11 of the 22 rated plans in the county earn 4 stars or higher.

Ratings matter for two practical reasons. First, they're a decent proxy for how much friction you'll experience — plans with chronically low ratings tend to generate more denials, more paperwork, and more phone calls. Second, if a 5-star plan is available in your county, Medicare allows you to switch into it once per year outside normal enrollment windows. Ratings shouldn't be the only filter, but we'd think hard before choosing a plan under 3.5 stars when 4+ star alternatives cover the same doctors.

The network question: will your doctors be covered?

This is the question that actually decides which plan is right, and it's the one most people skip. Medicare Advantage plans in Utah are built around provider networks — most commonly HMO, HMO-POS, or PPO structures — and along the Wasatch Front those networks are typically anchored by either Intermountain Health or University of Utah Health facilities and physician groups.

For Sandy-area residents, that means checking your specific hospital preference — Alta View, Intermountain Medical Center in Murray, St. Mark's, or University of Utah Health locations — plus your primary care physician and every specialist you see. An HMO plan generally requires you to stay in-network except for emergencies; a PPO lets you go out-of-network at a higher cost. Before any client enrolls, we run every provider on their list against the plan's current directory, because 'my doctor probably takes it' is how people end up with a plan they resent by February.

The drug list: same medications, very different bills

Every MA-PD plan has its own formulary — the list of drugs it covers, the tier each drug sits on, and which pharmacies get preferred pricing. Two neighbors taking the same five medications can pay hundreds of dollars a year apart purely because of which plan each one picked.

The good news for 2026: your out-of-pocket spending on covered Part D drugs is capped at $2,100 for the year. Hit that number and you pay $0 for covered drugs the rest of the year. But the cap doesn't make plan choice irrelevant — the plan still decides how fast and how painfully you get there, through tiers, deductibles (up to $615 standard in 2026, though many Salt Lake County plans reduce or waive it on lower tiers), and pharmacy pricing. We match your exact medication list, doses included, against each candidate plan's formulary before recommending anything.

Extras: dental, vision, hearing — worth weighing, not leading with

Most Salt Lake County Advantage plans bundle benefits Original Medicare doesn't cover: dental cleanings and dentures, vision exams and eyewear allowances, hearing aids, fitness memberships, and over-the-counter allowances. These are real money — a decent hearing aid benefit alone can be worth thousands.

Our advice is to treat extras as the tiebreaker, not the headline. A generous dental allowance doesn't compensate for a plan that puts your cardiologist out-of-network or your insulin on a high tier. Get the doctors and drugs right first; then let the extras break the tie between finalists.

What about Special Needs Plans (SNPs)?

Seventeen of Salt Lake County's 41 plans are Special Needs Plans — plans designed for specific situations. Chronic-condition SNPs (C-SNPs) serve people with conditions like diabetes, cardiovascular disorders, or chronic heart failure; Dual-eligible SNPs (D-SNPs) serve people who qualify for both Medicare and Medicaid. These plans tailor their benefits, provider networks, and drug formularies to their population, and often include enhanced care coordination.

If you have a qualifying chronic condition or Medicaid eligibility, a SNP can be meaningfully better than a general plan — but eligibility is verified, and the fit still depends on networks and formularies. It's worth a conversation rather than a guess.

When you can enroll or switch

The Annual Enrollment Period (AEP) runs October 15 through December 7 every year — that's when anyone on Medicare can join, drop, or switch Medicare Advantage and Part D plans for the following January 1. If you're already in an Advantage plan, the Medicare Advantage Open Enrollment Period (January 1 – March 31) gives you one additional switch — to a different Advantage plan or back to Original Medicare with a drug plan.

Outside those windows, Special Enrollment Periods open when life changes: moving out of your plan's service area, losing employer coverage, gaining Medicaid or Extra Help eligibility, or your plan leaving the county. And if you're new to Medicare, your 7-month Initial Enrollment Period around your 65th birthday is your first chance to choose.

A simple, honest way to choose

List your doctors and preferred hospital. List every prescription with its dose. Name the pharmacy you actually use. Then compare plans in this order: network first, drug coverage second, out-of-pocket maximum third, extras fourth, premium last. It's the reverse of how plans are advertised, and it's the order that predicts whether you'll be happy in July, not just in January.

Frequently asked questions

How many Medicare Advantage plans are available in Salt Lake County in 2026?

41 plans — 24 standard MA-PD plans and 17 Special Needs Plans — according to the CMS Medicare Plan Landscape for CY2026.

Are there $0-premium Medicare Advantage plans in Sandy, Utah?

Yes. 17 of the 24 Salt Lake County MA-PD plans have a $0 monthly plan premium for 2026. You still pay the Part B premium ($202.90 standard in 2026) plus plan copays.

Does a $0-premium plan mean free healthcare?

No. You pay the Part B premium, plus copays and coinsurance up to the plan's out-of-pocket maximum — which ranges from $3,500 to $9,250 across 2026 Salt Lake County plans.

What's the highest-rated Medicare Advantage plan in Salt Lake County?

The top overall CMS star rating available in the county for 2026 is 4.5 stars, and 11 of the 22 rated plans earn 4 stars or better. Ratings are one input — network and drug fit still come first.

Do Salt Lake County Advantage plans cover Intermountain and University of Utah doctors?

Networks vary by plan — many are anchored by one system or the other. Before enrolling, verify your specific doctors and hospital against the plan's current directory rather than assuming.

Can I switch plans if I choose wrong?

Yes — during the fall Annual Enrollment Period (Oct 15 – Dec 7) for the following year, or during the Medicare Advantage Open Enrollment Period (Jan 1 – Mar 31) if you're already in an Advantage plan.
